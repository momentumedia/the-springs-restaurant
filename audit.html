The findings

What's wrong with the current site.

Nine issues, ordered by impact. Each one explained in plain language, with the technical detail below.

  • Critical

    Doesn't work well on mobile.

    Over 60% of people browsing the web are on their phone. If your site feels clunky on a phone, buttons too small to tap, images misaligned, menus hard to read, they leave within seconds and book somewhere else. A reservation lost on mobile is a table gone that night.

    TechnicalWordPress platform theme with limited mobile-first control. Tap targets inconsistent with the 44px accessibility minimum. Menu images and event submenus don't reflow cleanly below 768px. No responsive typography system. Estimated Lighthouse mobile score around 40 to 55.

  • Critical

    The site loads too slowly.

    The current site takes several seconds to show anything meaningful. Most visitors give up after 3 seconds, and slow sites also rank lower on Google. Which makes the discoverability problem worse. Every second of load time is a booking or inquiry you may not get.

    TechnicalWordPress plus plugin stack loads large render-blocking JavaScript before any content paints. Images served as full-resolution JPGs without WebP or AVIF, and without responsive srcset. No first-party CDN. Core Web Vitals (LCP, CLS) likely failing Google's thresholds.

  • Critical

    Invisible on Google & AI search.

    When someone searches "fine dining London Ontario" or "wedding venue in a church London ON," your site doesn't show up the way it should. When someone asks ChatGPT or Google's AI to recommend a restaurant, your business often doesn't come up. New guests can't find you no matter how good the food is. They go to whoever does show up first.

    TechnicalNo structured data (JSON-LD for Restaurant, LocalBusiness, or OpeningHoursSpecification). Title tags and meta descriptions are platform defaults, not tuned for London ON or event queries. JS-rendered content and CDN challenge pages block many AI crawlers (ChatGPT Browse, Perplexity, Google SGE). No sitemap.xml reliably submitted.

  • High Priority

    The design doesn't match the building.

    You operate out of a restored historic church. The space is stunning, arches, beams, natural light, intimate private room. Stepping inside feels premium. The website, unfortunately, looks like a generic restaurant template. That gap costs you first-time guests and wedding couples who judge based on vibe before they read a single word.

    TechnicalBuilder-style WordPress theme with limited brand customization. No display-serif typography pairing. No editorial layout or whitespace discipline. No scroll animations or micro-interactions. Colour palette and type system don't carry through the restored-church / farm-to-table identity.

  • High Priority

    No clear path to reserve or inquire.

    A visitor lands on the homepage. What do you want them to do? Book a table? Ask about a wedding? Send a celebration-of-life inquiry? Right now the path splits four ways into submenus and nothing signals what matters most. Without one clear next step, people just close the tab.

    TechnicalReserve link lives in the top nav only, no sticky CTA on scroll, no hero conversion button above the fold. Event inquiries fragmented across Weddings, Corporate, Bridal Showers, and Celebrations of Life submenus without a unified inquiry form. Phone number not consistently tel:-linked.

  • High Priority

    Your photos aren't used well.

    The plates photograph beautifully. The church interior photographs beautifully. Real wedding setups, the tent, the private room, they all photograph beautifully. But right now the site shows them small, in thumbnail grids, without editorial weight. A restaurant that looks this good deserves full-bleed hero shots and galleries that make people want to walk in.

    TechnicalNo full-bleed hero imagery. Gallery laid out as small uniform tiles with no lightbox or zoom. Instagram feed not deeply integrated. Event photography from real weddings not surfaced prominently. No masonry or editorial rhythm to the image presentation.

  • Medium Priority

    Hours & location are buried.

    Your hours are complicated. Thursday and Friday have split lunch and dinner service. Other days you open at 4:30pm. People checking if they can swing by for dinner tonight should see that at a glance, from any page. Same with the Springbank Drive address and phone number. One glance away, every page.

    TechnicalHours shown on home page body text only, not in persistent footer or header. No schema.org/OpeningHoursSpecification markup, so Google Maps, Siri, and voice assistants may show incomplete hours. Phone number not tel:-linked across all pages. No closed-day visual indicator.

  • Medium Priority

    Event pricing is hidden on a second website.

    You have clear, reasonable set menu tiers: Lunch from $65, Dinner from $79 to $115. But those prices live on a separate Square site, not on your main website. Couples planning a wedding never see the numbers, so they assume you're out of reach and don't submit an inquiry. A price that would have won the booking never even gets seen.

    TechnicalMain site (thespringsrestaurant.com) and Square site (thespringsrestaurant.square.site) operate as two separate surfaces. Hours, menus, and pricing must be maintained in two places. Guests who land on one often bounce between both and lose the thread.

  • Medium Priority

    The platform limits what's possible.

    Being locked into a template platform means every future idea, a seasonal campaign, a nicer gallery, a new event landing page, anything unique to The Springs, has to squeeze into someone else's box. A proper custom site grows with the business instead of constraining it.

    TechnicalWordPress + plugin stack with theme constraints that prevent custom components, animations, or bespoke page types. Limited control over head tags, robots.txt, and redirects. Recurring platform and plugin licensing fees. No native way to integrate modern build tools or custom APIs (e.g. Square Orders or third-party inquiry workflows).

What's working

A few things worth keeping.

These transfer directly to the new site.

  • A building that tells a story

    A restored historic church, a main venue that seats up to 85, a private room for 35, and an outdoor tent that extends beyond. Very few venues in London can say any of that. The new site leads with it.

  • Genuine photography

    Real plates, real weddings, real corporate setups, the actual room. No stock photos needed. The new site gives them the editorial stage they deserve.

  • Focused positioning

    Casual fine-dining bistro. Farm to table. Events for every kind of gathering. A clear two-part story, easy for guests to grasp on first visit.
